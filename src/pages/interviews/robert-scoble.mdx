# Robert Scoble

**Summary**: Discovery interview with Robert Scoble (October 30, 2023) — a world-renowned technology evangelist, blogger, and author of four tech trend books including "Age of Context," who has had a front-row seat to Silicon Valley's evolution since 1978 and whose vision of AI "contextual committees" represents the future of context-aware computing.

**Sources**: CULTIVATING CLARITY.md

**Last updated**: 2026-04-14

---

## Background

Robert Scoble has been immersed in Silicon Valley technology since childhood. His career arc reads as a chronological history of modern tech.

### The Early Years (1978-1990s)
- **1978**: Learned to assemble Apple II motherboards in his mother's kitchen in Cupertino, California (calls himself "Apple's first child laborer")
- His father, an engineer, bought a house in Cupertino in 1971 — before Apple was founded
- Worked at a family-owned consumer electronics store in the 1980s, learning about advertising and consumer purchasing decisions (e.g., why someone chooses a $200 camera over $2,000, or how they decided between Beta and VHS)
- **1989**: Spotted Steve Wozniak in the parking lot of West Valley Community College, struck up a friendship, and studied with him every morning in the cafeteria. Convinced Wozniak to donate $40,000 worth of Macs to the journalism department
- Moved to San Jose State, where he set up 50 Macs for teachers (replacing an expensive consultant), redesigned the school newspaper using QuarkXPress, and got his fingers into Adobe Photoshop

### The Media/Microsoft Era (1993-2006)
- Joined a small programming magazine (Basic Pro) as an intern — just before Microsoft released Visual Basic. The magazine grew from 6 to 200 people
- Met Bill Gates, did exclusive interviews, organized conferences that grew from hundreds to thousands
- **1994**: Witnessed the birth of web development from day one through the magazine
- **1998**: Was present when Google came to pitch the magazine when they were "brand new"
- Started blogging in December 2000; attended Woz's Super Bowl party in 2001 with John Draper "Captain Crunch"
- **2003-2006**: Hired by Microsoft's Vic Gundotra (who later funded Android at Google) to teach 1,100 evangelists how to do community engagement. Started Channel 9 — buying a $250 Sony camera and interviewing 600 Microsoft employees "from Bill Gates to the janitor." The Economist wrote a full page article claiming he "humanized Microsoft"

### Post-Microsoft (2006-present)
- Worked at Fast Company and Rackspace
- Launched or featured early versions of Flipboard, Cloudera, Tesla (had the first ride in the very first Tesla — Elon Musk gave him a ride before giving his best friend one, because they were racing)
- Wrote books on spatial computing and "The Age of Context"
- Encountered augmented reality 12-13 years before the interview when he visited Metaio in Germany (later acquired by Apple, with the CTO building Apple's Vision Pro strategy)
- Currently runs the "Unaligned" podcast focused on AI

## Key Concepts

### "Humans Are Patterns"
From his conversation with the founder of Otter (formerly a Google Maps engineer):
> "Humans are patterns, and we're studying those patterns."

Google Maps understood that a user's experience differs fundamentally when entering a shopping store versus a sports arena. This insight — that context shapes every digital interaction — inspired Scoble to write "Age of Context."

### AI Contextual Committees
Scoble's most forward-looking concept, and his main contribution to the book's thesis:

His favorite ChatGPT prompt is: "Please show me all points of view on X." This works as a four-stage process:

1. **Build personas** for each point of view on a topic
2. **Join those personas into a panel discussion** — a debate club or group that hashes things out
3. The AI produces a **rich multi-perspective debate** (e.g., asking about Austin barbecue yields a chef, restaurant critic, locals with different views)
4. You end up with **much greater contextual awareness**

The vision extends further: we will have **dynamic contextual committees that change depending on our context**:
- Deciding on barbecue? → Committee of food experts, critics, locals
- Doing physics homework? → Committee of physicists, teachers, practitioners
- Driving past historical landmarks? → Committee of historians, architects, local experts

> "Why only have one person's point of view? Why not have a committee?... And why only have one committee? Why not have a committee for every fucking thing?"

### Response to Ian's NeuroKick Demo
When Ian demonstrated his AI leadership development tool, Scoble was immediately energized:

> "It's interesting to have a little coach listening for a very specific thing, right? And to have it be that fast is really cool."

He connected it to his own psychiatrist using ChatGPT for therapy session support — "the same engine you guys are both using but doing completely different things." Both are narrow, context-specific applications of the same underlying technology, each producing entirely different results based on the context they're given.

Scoble's follow-up question was prescient: "Can it figure out 10 things to look for?" — pushing Ian toward scaling the tool's contextual awareness.

### Gaussian Splats and Spatial Computing
Scoble's vision of the near future includes AR glasses with displays showing virtual scenes rendered from "Gaussian splats" — real-life data captured into 3D space and rendered in real-time. Combined with AI assistants and contextual committees, this creates a future where context-aware intelligence is literally overlaid on the physical world.

### ChatGPT's Mind-Blowing Multimodal Capabilities
Even the three co-founders of Siri (who ran the biggest AI group in the world in the 80s at SRI International, "where the internet was born") said ChatGPT shocked them. Scoble's examples: pointing a camera at the Carmel Mission Basilica and getting facts from an image, or aiming at a spice rack and getting all spices identified. "If this is the worst it's ever going to be, it's mind-blowing."

## Relevance to Contextual Intelligence

Scoble contributes the *technological trajectory* of context. His career is essentially a timeline of how technology has progressively gained the ability to understand and respond to context — from static computing to the internet to mobile to spatial computing to AI. His vision of contextual committees represents the next frontier: AI systems that dynamically assemble multi-perspective understanding based on the user's current situation.

His insight that the same AI engine produces entirely different results depending on the context it's given (leadership coaching vs. therapy support vs. barbecue recommendations) is a practical demonstration of the book's core thesis: context determines value.
