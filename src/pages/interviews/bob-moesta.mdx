# Bob Moesta

**Summary**: Discovery interview with Bob Moesta (November 17, 2023) — co-creator of the Jobs to Be Done theory, master of consumer behavior, and a titan of innovation who applies his engineering mindset and deep empathy to decode the complex narratives behind why people make choices. His contributions on the three layers of language, demand-side thinking, and the switching moment are among the book's most actionable insights.

## Background

Bob Moesta is best known as the co-creator of the [Jobs to Be Done](/concepts/jobs-to-be-done) theory. He has spent decades researching consumer behavior, applying an engineering mindset to unpack decision-making. His mentors include Dr. Deming and Dr. Taguchi — both instrumental in the Toyota Production System that also shaped Ian's early career.

Bob is the author of:
- **"Demand-Side Sales 101: Stop Selling and Help Your Customers Make Progress"** — Reframes sales as coaching customers toward progress
- **"Learning to Build"** — Codifies the learning process for building new products
- Contributor to **"Shape Up"** (with Ryan Singer from Basecamp) — Challenges traditional scrum/agile methodologies

Bob has extreme ADHD and uses different physical spaces to think in different ways: "Context drives how I think." He lost 100 pounds over three years — not for the output (weight loss) but for the outcome (skiing with his adult kids).

## Key Concepts

### Three Layers of Language
See full page: [Three Layers of Language](/concepts/three-layers-of-language)

Bob describes three layers at which human communication operates:

1. **The Pablum Layer** — Surface-level conversation where precision is lacking and meaningful engagement is rare. "It's like when people ask, 'How was your day?' and you reply, 'It was good.' But what does that actually mean?" Bob finds it "absurd" that LLMs are being built on this shallow level of language.

2. **The Fantasy/Nightmare Layer** — Where things are exaggerated, either worse or better than they actually are, "almost like a commercial." People operate here when describing what they want or fear in idealized/catastrophized terms.

3. **The Causal Layer** — "About understanding the sequence of events, the cause and effect. That's where you find the truth." This is the layer the [Jobs to Be Done](/concepts/jobs-to-be-done) theory targets — the actual reasons behind choices, uncovered by tracing the timeline of events.

### The Switching Moment
See full page: [The Switching Moment](/concepts/the-switching-moment)

> "The switching moment is where the memories lie."

Bob's profound insight: people don't remember mundane purchases because there was no biological trigger. But decisions involving risk — like switching brands, changing jobs, buying a house — are "rich with emotional and biological markers" (dopamine, serotonin, cortisol).

The implication for customer discovery: focus your interviews on moments of *change*. When someone switched from one product/service/job to another, that transition point reveals the unconscious motivations that drove the decision.

> "The conscious mind is like the tip of an iceberg. The subconscious, below the waterline, reveals itself when people make changes."

### "Bitchin' Ain't Switching"

A memorable maxim: complaining about a product or situation is *not* the same as being willing to change. Voicing dissatisfaction without the push for actual change leads nowhere. There must be sufficient **push** (pain with the current situation) and **pull** (attraction to a new solution) to overcome the forces of **habit** (comfort with the known) and **anxiety** (fear of the unknown).

### Demand-Side vs. Supply-Side Thinking
See full page: [Demand Side vs Supply Side](/concepts/demand-side-vs-supply-side)

Bob insists that **demand exists irrespective of supply**. The critical question is not "what can we build?" but "what progress are people trying to make?"

**The Southern New Hampshire University Case Study**: In 2010, they had 2,500 students with only 50 in remote learning. President Paul LeBlanc asked: "How many people want to go back to school but can't?" This uncovered massive latent demand for accessible education. By reimagining the system for online learners, the university became the largest in the world, with a campus extending to Africa, reducing college costs by 70%.

> "Innovation comes from the customers."

### Trade-offs vs. Compromises
See full page: [Trade Offs vs Compromises](/concepts/trade-offs-vs-compromises)

A critical distinction most people conflate:

- **Compromise**: You have an expectation of X, then settle for X minus 2. You settle — and inevitably feel dissatisfied.
- **Trade-off**: You consciously give something up to get something else. Expectations are realigned; satisfaction comes from informed choice.

Understanding which customers are making trade-offs vs. compromises reveals entirely different opportunity spaces.

### Output vs. Outcome

Bob's weight loss example crystallizes this distinction:
- **Output**: Losing 100 pounds (the measurable result)
- **Outcome**: Being able to ski with his adult kids (the meaningful life change)

The same output can lead to vastly different outcomes for different people. Product teams must focus on outcomes, not outputs. "Your questioning strategy focuses on outcomes, bypassing the standard outputs."

### Functional, Social, and Emotional Dimensions
See full page: [Functional Social Emotional Dimensions](/concepts/functional-social-emotional-dimensions)

Three energy types drive purchasing decisions:

1. **Functional** (mechanical energy) — "My computer runs slow"
2. **Social** (chemical energy) — "My boss will think I'm incompetent"
3. **Emotional** (electrical energy) — "I'm afraid I'll lose my job"

These transform between states: a functional need evolves into social concern, which builds into emotional intensity, eventually triggering a behavior change (buying a new car, switching jobs, etc.).

### Customer vs. Consumer

A critical distinction: the **customer** buys the product, but the **consumer** gets value from it. In toys: "If I built the toys parents wanted, kids wouldn't play with them. And if I built the toys kids wanted, parents wouldn't buy them." Product design must satisfy both.

### Desirability, Viability, and Feasibility

The three pillars of successful product development:
- **Desirability** — Do people want it?
- **Viability** — Can the business sustain it?
- **Feasibility** — Can it be built?

Desirability is the most often neglected — teams assume technical feasibility equals market desire.

### "No Is Way Better Than Yes"

Bob's counterintuitive interview technique: provoke "no" responses to go deeper. He deliberately plays things back incorrectly to prompt corrections, "essentially prototyping understanding in real-time." This leverages human nature's tendency to correct inaccuracies, allowing deeper exploration of experience and opinion.

> "It's really not hard to ask the questions if you're curious. It's hard to get past the ego to look stupid to ask the question."

### The Steak vs. Pizza Example

Context determines value more than the product itself. People say they like both steak and pizza, but:
- **Pizza context**: Rushing, kids need to eat before bedtime, grab something quick
- **Steak context**: Celebration, big meal with wine and company

The product is secondary to the situation in which it's consumed.
