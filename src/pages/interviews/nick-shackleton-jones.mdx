# Nick Shackleton-Jones

**Summary**: Discovery interview with Nick Shackleton-Jones (October 13, 2023) — a trailblazer in learning and development who created the Affective Context Model, demonstrating that learning is fundamentally an emotional process and that information gains significance only within an affective context.

**Sources**: CULTIVATING CLARITY.md

**Last updated**: 2026-04-14

---

## Background

Nick Shackleton-Jones is the author of "How People Learn" and the creator of the [Affective Context Model](/concepts/affective-context), a framework proposing that learning is the process by which people attach emotional (affective) sense to information. He has over a decade of experience in learning and development, working with large organizations and running his own consultancy.

His current work involves introducing human-centered processes into organizational learning — starting with understanding what people struggle with, care about, and need, rather than the traditional approach of loading courses with content. His programs "look nothing like any educational program you've seen before, because they don't focus on content."

## The Siemens Experiment — A Turning Point

Early in his career as a psychology lecturer, Nick believed that applying cognitive psychology, memory research, and social psychology through technology would transform learning. While working for Siemens Communications, he tested this hypothesis rigorously:

They created the same learning module in 5-6 different formats:
- One version incorporated all the theory: interaction, multiple levels of encoding, narratives
- At the other extreme: just plain text

The hypothesis: applying learning theory and cognitive psychology would produce significantly better outcomes.

**The shocking result**: The individuals who remembered the most were those who just read the plain text document.

This was a "horrifying finding" that forced Nick to "reset my understanding." He compared the experience to "emerging from an era that only had the appearance of science and understanding, somewhat akin to homeopathy. Not really medicine, just many people convincing themselves that this stuff works."

This revelation led him to develop the Affective Context Model — moving beyond the illusion that learning is about information transfer to understanding that it's about emotional engagement.

## Key Concepts

### The Affective Context Model
See full page: [Affective Context](/concepts/affective-context)

Nick proposes that "learning is the process by which people attach emotional (or affective) sense to information." This challenges the prevailing misconception of learning as mere transmission of knowledge.

Key implications:
- Information alone is meaningless without emotional context
- People remember what they *cared about*, not what they were told
- The enduring memories of education are moments imbued with emotional significance — joy, curiosity, or even discomfort
- For knowledge to truly assimilate, it must be accompanied by an "affective resonance that renders it meaningful"

### Experience Specifications vs. Functional Specifications

Nick's most immediately actionable insight for product development:

A learning management system (or any product) might meet every functional specification — checking all the boxes on paper. But if it fails to address what people *care about* ("Was that a cool thing to use? Was it fun? Did I enjoy it? Did it solve my problem right here, right now?"), it will fail in practice.

> "This is the fundamental common mismatch of things being considered out of context. If you assess context from a computational standpoint, you won't be able to understand why it's not interfacing with human beings who are assessing context from an emotional, affective standpoint."

The remedy: **prioritize experience specifications over functional ones**. A system must not only work correctly but also engage users emotionally and contextually.

### How Different Entities Encode Context

Nick outlined three fundamentally different ways of encoding environmental context:

1. **Engineers**: Define surroundings through measurable dimensions (height, width, temperature)
2. **Computers**: Interpret the world through patterns of light and dark, matching patterns against shadows
3. **Humans**: Rely *solely* on emotional reactions to understand context

A human driver cares about a tree because hitting it would total the car. They care about a cat because they care about cats. They care about children on the sidewalk because they care about children. A computer simply sees patterns of light and shade — "it's not a human being, so computers don't care about a cat."

The tiger example: "If you were driving a car and you see a tiger, you will be like, Oh my God, I just saw a tiger in the street. You tell that story to everybody, your whole day, possibly a whole month, will be skewed by that. A computer will say, Tiger, not interesting, and move on."

### The Ethics of Self-Driving Cars

Building on this framework, Nick addressed the challenge of programming ethics into autonomous vehicles. His position is stark:

> "There are no rules for ethics, because ethics is based on how you feel."

He references Michael Sandel's trolley problem: you might rationally conclude to save five by sacrificing one, but if you feel a connection to that one person and something tells you those five are evil, "the decision is going to go a whole different way, because we act in every situation according to what we are feeling."

Until machines develop a genuine capacity to *care*, they cannot be ethical in any meaningful sense. Current approaches (including LLMs) are simulations of care, not the thing itself.

> "It is simply impossible to have a machine that can be ethical in the same way that you and I do... What you can do, though, is just a simulation of it."

### User-Centered Design and Don Norman

Nick advocates for applying Don Norman's user-centered design principles to learning:

1. **Define** — Pinpoint specific outcomes; have direct conversations with customers to unearth challenges
2. **Discover** — Find out what learners are struggling with, what they care about, why they come to work
3. **Design** — Build something that actually assists with those challenges

This is the synthesis of top-down business goals with bottom-up user needs — the same approach Henry Ford actually took (understanding the underlying need for *speed*, not literally asking for faster horses).

### Formal vs. Informal Learning

Nick highlights the distinction between formal learning (curriculum-driven, content-heavy) and informal learning (organic, context-embedded). The latter is far more effective because it embeds learning within a framework of relevance and immediacy. The forgetting curve (Ebbinghaus) demonstrates the limitations of rote memorization, while contextually rich experiences create lasting memories.

## Seven Key Takeaways (from the book's summary)

1. **Experience over Functionality** — Prioritize how users *feel* about a product, not just what it does
2. **Affective Context Model** — Emotional resonance determines what we learn and remember
3. **User-Centered Design** — Engage directly with users before building anything
4. **Emotional and Contextual Relevance** — Computers can't replicate human ethical decision-making
5. **Bridging Business and User Needs** — Synthesize top-down objectives with bottom-up insights
6. **Challenging Traditional Models** — Content-centric education and development fail without emotional engagement
7. **Context in Memory and Decision-Making** — What we remember is what we cared about
