# Michael Levin

**Summary**: Discovery interview with Michael Levin (February 20, 2024) — Distinguished Professor of Biology at Tufts University, director of the Allen Discovery Center, and a visionary whose work on bioelectricity, biological memory, cognitive light cones, and universal hacking provides the deepest and most philosophically rich perspective on context in the entire book.

**Sources**: CULTIVATING CLARITY.md

**Last updated**: 2026-04-14

## Background

Michael Levin holds the Vannevar Bush endowed Chair in the Biology department at Tufts University and directs both the Allen Discovery Center at Tufts and the Tufts Center for Regenerative and Developmental Biology. His work focuses on understanding the biophysical mechanisms that implement decision-making during complex pattern regulation, and harnessing endogenous bioelectric dynamics toward rational control of growth and form.

His recent honors include the Scientist of Vision award and the Distinguished Scholar Award.

Ian discovered Levin through the Lex Fridman podcast in October 2022, where Levin discussed bioelectricity as "a much more ancient ability of cells to form networks for solving different kinds of problems." All components of neurons — ion channels, neurotransmitter machinery, electrical synapses — are much older than brains and neurons. "Evolution discovered early on that electrical networks are excellent for memory retention, information integration across distances, and various optimization tasks, such as image recognition, long before the existence of brains."

## Key Concepts

### Memory as Dynamic, Context-Dependent Process
See full page: [Memory As Dynamic Process](/concepts/memory-as-dynamic-process)

Levin's first major contribution: memory is not static storage. From conventional neuroscience, we already know:
- Every time you recall a memory, you modify it ("There is no such thing as read-only memory")
- No structures in the brain remain unchanged for a lifetime
- Memory constantly writes and rewrites itself, strengthening while also modifying

But Levin goes much deeper with the caterpillar-butterfly example.

### The Caterpillar-Butterfly Problem

This is one of the book's most powerful illustrations of context-dependent intelligence:

**Step 1 — Memory persistence across substrate change**: Caterpillars can be trained to eat leaves on a specific colored background. After metamorphosis (where the brain is thoroughly remodeled, most connections dissolved, and most cells die), the butterfly/moth will go to that same colored background to look for food. Memory persists despite radical hardware changes.

**Step 2 — The deeper insight nobody talks about**: Butterflies don't eat leaves. They drink nectar. So storing a memory of "leaves associated with a colored disc" is useless to a butterfly. For the memory to be useful, two things must happen:

1. **Generalization**: The memory must be generalized from "leaves" to the category "food" — and "generalizing from particulars to categories is intelligence"
2. **Remapping**: The memory must be remapped onto a completely new architecture — from a soft-bodied robot in 2D space to a hard-bodied robot in 3D space, from crawling muscles to wing-flapping muscles

> "You cannot just keep things the same. They make little sense in the new context. There's your link to context — biology is all about the current context and future opportunities, not a fixed meaning of messages from the past."

### The Chip-in-the-Toaster Thought Experiment

To illustrate how different biological memory is from computer memory: "I'm going to pop a chip out of your laptop and stick it in my toaster and everything from your laptop, all the recipes about how to cook bread, now they make perfect sense in the toaster? We have nothing like that yet."

Computer data is tied to a specific interpreter. Biological information is inherently interoperable because biology deals with unreliable hardware from day one — "you don't know how many cells you're going to have; you know very little that you can take for granted."

> "All of your information, both morphological and behavioral, is remappable because it never made the assumption that the hardware was going to stay constant."

### Memory as Communication Between Temporal Slices

> "As living beings, at any given point in time, we don't have access to the past; what we have access to are the engrams — the memory traces that the past has left in your brain or body."

Memory is a **message left for you from your past self**. This becomes concrete with brain-damaged patients who can't form new memories — they leave themselves notes on pads of paper saying "I know you just woke up, but here's what you need to know." The rest of us do exactly the same thing, but internalized.

At every moment, "we dynamically reconstruct, build, and maintain the story of yourself and your world that defines you and your commitments to what you should do next." This process gives rise to consciousness — "the sense of being a self-creating sense-making system felt from the inside."

Collective intelligences use the same pattern: ants and termites leave chemical messages on the sand they crawl around on. "We all use some kind of substrate to keep track of what we're doing as a collective intelligence evolving forward through time."

### Cognitive Light Cone
See full page: [Cognitive Light Cone](/concepts/cognitive-light-cone)

Levin proposes a continuum of goal-having based on cybernetics (Wiener and Rosenbluth), which he calls the **spectrum of persuadability**:

> "The cognitive light cone is the size of the biggest goal you could possibly pursue."

The spectrum:

| Entity | Goal Size | Time Horizon | Spatial Range |
|--------|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| Single cell | Metabolic/physiological | Minutes | Size of a cell |
| Embryo | Anatomical (build organs) | Days-weeks | Organism-scale |
| Animal (dog) | Behavioral | Hours back/forward | ~100 yards |
| Human | Social, linguistic, transcendent | Beyond lifespan | Global |
| Bodhisattva | Every sentient being | Unbounded | Universal |

How to know something has goals: "A goal is something that when you perturb the system and try to keep it from its goal, you see that it reroutes and fights you and tries to get there."

Key insight: **goals scale with collective organization**. Put cells together in a particular way, and the collective has bigger goals than individual cells. Put rocks in a pile, and the pile has exactly the same goal capacity as individual rocks. "The reason we call one alive and one not alive is simply because the things we call alive are good at scaling goals."

Unique human trait: having goals bigger than your lifespan. "If you're a goldfish, all of your goals are achievable within your lifetime because their goals are about 20 minutes forward... But if you're a human, many of your goals are probably not achievable."

### Universal Hacking
See full page: [Universal Hacking](/concepts/universal-hacking)

> "Everything is trying to hack everything else, and by hacking I don't mean in the negative sense of exploitation... but rather, using whatever your perspective of a system is to control it, to get it to do something."

The key distinction: in biology, there is no manual, no "correct way to use the system." Every agent must build its own understanding of how things work from its own perspective. "Nobody tells you where the control knobs are."

Levin references the paper "The Child as a Hacker" — children come into the world without knowing the "right way" to do anything. They build internal models and "will subvert anything you plan... because they don't have any allegiance to your categories of how things are meant to be used."

This applies to all agents: "Being an agent means you have to have your own point of view from which you develop a version of how to cut up the world into functional pieces."

### Observer-Dependent Context
See full page: [Observer Dependent Context](/concepts/observer-dependent-context)

> "Context is in the eye of the beholder. It is a best guess. It is a set of affordances where you look around and you say, well, I can sit on this, and I can eat this other thing, and I can hide under this other thing."

You, as the observer, decide what your context is and how you're going to see it. "If you're good at it, you'll have a very great adaptive life. And if you're not good at it, then you'll leave a lot on the table."

### Cognitive Glue — But with Caution

Biology has evolved principles of "cognitive glue" that enable the scaling of collective goals. However, Levin warned against directly copying biological principles for human systems:

> "When you as a giant collective go spend a day rock climbing, you're going to leave a whole bunch of your skin cells on that wall... But nobody asked those cells if they were willing to die on the side of a rock cliff."

Biological collectives gain capabilities at the collective level without tracking individual welfare. "In the political arena, that kind of forced collectivism has been tried a number of times in history and it always works the same way — human rights disasters and drastic reduction of human flourishing."

> "We need to be looking for optimal policies for scaling collective intelligence, not necessarily copying what biology does, because I don't think biology is tracking all the values that we have (or should have)."

## Relevance to Contextual Intelligence

Levin provides the deepest, most philosophically rich perspective on context in the entire book. His contributions are threefold:

1. **Context is dynamic and observer-dependent**: There is no objective, fixed context — each agent constructs its own version based on its perspective, capabilities, and goals.

2. **Information must be generalized and remapped**: Just as the caterpillar's memory of leaves must become the butterfly's understanding of food, customer insights must be generalized beyond specific products to underlying needs, and remapped to new solution spaces.

3. **Collective intelligence requires care about values**: While biology's cognitive glue principles can inspire team alignment, they must be tempered by human values — particularly individual dignity and consent. This connects directly to [Ori Freiman](/interviews/ori-freiman)'s ethical framework.
