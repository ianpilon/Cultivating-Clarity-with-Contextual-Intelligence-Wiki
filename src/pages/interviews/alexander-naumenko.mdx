# Alexander Naumenko

**Summary**: Discovery interview with Alexander Naumenko (November 5, 2023) — a linguist and AI theorist whose work on context-dependent learning, intelligence through differences, and coherence constraints offers a rigorous theoretical framework for how context shapes meaning, memory, and cognition.

**Sources**: CULTIVATING CLARITY.md

**Last updated**: 2026-04-14

---

## Background

Alexander Naumenko is the author of "Theory of Language as of Reflection of World Modeling by Human Intelligence" and the Substack paper "Memory." Ian discovered him through a serendipitous encounter on Twitter (now X) when Carlos E. Perez posted that "vector embeddings are just like episodic memories." Ian replied about affective neuroscience and memory, and Alexander jumped into the thread with a link to his paper on memory.

Alexander's journey into AI research was sparked by work with Braintree, further fueled during COVID lockdown (which drove him to focus inward), and deepened by the war in his homeland of Ukraine. During this period of adversity, he developed his ideas on language's components, arriving at an epiphany: "Objects have properties. Actions change properties. Properties are all we need."

## Key Concepts

### Intelligence Through Differences
See full page: [Intelligence Through Differences](/concepts/intelligence-through-differences)

Alexander's central theory challenges conventional views of intelligence. Rather than operating through pattern matching and similarity recognition, he argues that intelligence fundamentally works through **differentiation** — identifying and managing differences.

> "My approach to intelligence really centers on the ability to handle differences. This shifts the computation behind cognition to focus on comparisons rather than mathematical operations or calculations."

### The 20 Questions Analogy

The game of 20 Questions perfectly illustrates this theory. A player narrows down possibilities through systematic exclusion:

- Each yes/no question eliminates a swath of non-relevant options
- The process focuses on *differences* (what the object is NOT) rather than similarities
- This binary search approach is far more efficient for quick, accurate identification than comparing against lists of similar items
- With 20 binary questions, you can theoretically distinguish among over 1 million possibilities

This mechanism extrapolates to a broader understanding of intelligence: "Intelligence excels not by accumulating and comparing similarities but by discerning and managing differences."

### Context as Filter — The Net Metaphor

Alexander provided one of the most precise definitions of context in the book:

> "For me, context is all the objects that are available to the speaker and to the listener, and relationships among those objects. Language filters our focus through the properties of objects when we communicate."

His metaphor: references in language are not **harpoons** (pointing precisely at one thing) but **nets** that capture all fitting descriptions. "When we stack those nets like filters, what passes through is the meaning. So meaning depends always on context, always."

### Coherence Constraints

Alexander coined the term "coherence constraints" to explain how questions shape understanding:

> "Consider any question; it's essentially a declarative sentence missing one piece, highlighted by the question word. The rest of the question constrains what can fill that gap. Not just anything fits."

Example: asking about a tree's height demands a *measurement* in response. The question itself constrains the type of valid answer. Human intelligence uses questions as tools to fill knowledge gaps, "narrowing down possibilities like a binary search to pinpoint precise answers."

### Knowledge as Reward

Alexander characterized knowledge acquisition as the ultimate reward for an intelligent system. The satisfaction derived from filling knowledge gaps drives cognitive behavior. Using the example of an apple: "We don't consider all properties of an object at once; it depends on the context we're in. The same apple can be seen as food or as a makeshift weapon."

This context-dependence of perception reinforces that intelligence is not about accumulating all possible information but about focusing on the *relevant* differences in the current situation.

### Properties and Ranges — Simplifying AI Language Processing

Alexander's approach to AI focuses on **properties and their ranges** rather than objects and actions. This simplification could lead to more precise and meaningful human-AI interactions because it reduces ambiguity and focuses on what's truly important in any given context.

His theory of language components:
- **Objects** have properties
- **Actions** change properties  
- **Properties** are all we need

### Context-Dependent Learning and Memory

From Alexander's Substack paper "Memory" (November 2, 2023):

1. Memory and perception can be easily manipulated and distorted based on context and needs
2. We reduce information dimensions for faster, more efficient processing
3. Learning from others' mistakes is often overestimated
4. The role of learning is to help in future interactions with the world
5. Concepts frequently discussed together form tighter memory relationships, especially with emotions involved
6. Spaced learning is more efficient than single-session learning
7. Retrieved memories are rarely identical to the initial experience — irrelevant details are removed over time
8. Emotionally rich memories are stored faster and for longer periods

### The Role of Rehearsal

Alexander offered a unique insight on internal rehearsal: when we observe a scene with an apple tree and say "Those apples are yummy," we're not looking for weapons or shelter — we're hungry. "By rehearsing we impose our point of view on what we observe and start projecting, simplifying, and modeling the context according to our needs/goals."

Rehearsal serves as a mechanism for turning implicit needs into explicit representation, and for internalizing external information.

### Language and Memory — Sound Over Sight

A crucial finding: we are more likely to make mistakes remembering words that *sound* like other words than those that *look* like other words. The same is true for deaf people — they confuse words using similar hand shapes. Suppressing internal rehearsal (or hand movements for deaf people) significantly worsens the ability to remember text-based information.

## Connection to DSRP Thinking

Alexander's theory aligns with the [Dsrp Thinking](/concepts/dsrp-thinking) model (Distinctions, Systems, Relationships, and Perspectives) from Derek Cabrera and Laura Colosi's "Thinking at Every Desk." The emphasis on **distinctions** (differences) as foundational to cognition maps directly onto Naumenko's approach. Ian adopted DSRP in 2020, and finding Alexander's independently developed but converging theory was a powerful validation.

## Relevance to Contextual Intelligence

Naumenko contributes the *cognitive-linguistic* dimension of context. His work explains the mechanics of how context shapes meaning at the most fundamental level — through the filtering, constraining, and differentiating processes of language and thought. His theory suggests that both human and artificial intelligence can be improved by focusing on differentiation over similarity, properties over objects, and ranges over fixed categories.

For product innovation, this means customer discovery should focus on identifying *differences* — what distinguishes this customer's situation from others, what constraints narrow the solution space, and what properties of the problem are most salient in the current context.
