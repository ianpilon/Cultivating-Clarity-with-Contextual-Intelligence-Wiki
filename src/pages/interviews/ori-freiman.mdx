# Ori Freiman

**Summary**: Discovery interview with Ori Freiman (November 27, 2023) — a philosopher and scholar at the intersection of AI ethics and blockchain governance whose insights on trust, absolute vs. relativist ethics, consent, and the fluid nature of ethical meaning in the digital age provide the moral framework for contextual intelligence.

**Sources**: CULTIVATING CLARITY.md

**Last updated**: 2026-04-14

---

## Background

Ori Freiman holds formal education in philosophy and library science, with a career spanning blockchain governance, emerging technologies, and regulation. His PhD focused on trust in technology, with particular emphasis on blockchains and AI. His institutional affiliations include:

- **Ethics of AI Lab** at the University of Toronto
- **McMaster's Digital Society Lab** — exploring digital ethics, misinformation, and trust
- **Centre for International Governance Innovation (CIGI)** — researching trust and Central Bank Digital Currencies (CBDCs) in the Canadian context

Ian met Ori in November 2022 at Web3 & Blockchain World in Toronto, where Charles Hoskinson and Tamara Haasen were also present. Ori approached their table and introduced himself to Charles. Ian quickly photographed Ori's name badge before celebrity seekers interrupted, recognizing that ethics was "an often underrated and underappreciated aspect of the technological systems we're building."

## Key Concepts

### Context Influences the Meaning of Ethics

Ori's central contribution to the book:

> "Context is a very important factor in influencing meaning, but also interpreting meaning of anything that has to do with ethics, moral values, and with the principles we adopt, and the judgments that we have."

This insight extends context beyond product design into the realm of moral reasoning itself. What counts as ethical behavior shifts based on:
- Cultural context (different societies hold different values)
- Temporal context (social media went from democratic promise to societal threat)
- Technological context (new technologies evoke new moral reactions)
- Individual context (personal experiences shape moral intuitions)

### Absolute vs. Relativist Ethics

When Ian initially attributed a relativist stance to Ori, he was corrected firmly: "No, the contrary."

> "I adopt the stance that there are principles that are true everywhere for humanity to flourish. There are values I think that should be absolute. I'm not a relativist in the moral sense."

Ori's position: while he believes in liberalism to a degree (allowing people to choose their values), certain values are non-negotiable — particularly human dignity and how we treat humans. This is especially important in the context of AI and technology development, where the temptation to prioritize innovation over ethics is strong.

### The Evolution of Ethics in the Digital Age

Ori traced a shift in what "ethics" means in contemporary discourse:

**Traditional ethics**: Rooted in ancient Greek philosophy — "How best should I live my life?" Frameworks like utilitarianism and Kantian ethics provide general rules for determining right action.

**Contemporary ethics**: Now encompasses broader discussions about social justice, activism, concentration of power and wealth, individual freedom and autonomy. These concerns are "less about applying classical ethical theories and more about addressing the moral judgments, principles, and values relevant to today's technology-infused society."

Ori emphasized that traditional ethical theories are *insufficient* for addressing the complexities of modern technology. Societies must consciously choose and uphold values reflective of their identity and aspirations, turning ethics into policy through representative processes.

### Disinformation as an Ethical Absolute

Ori used organized disinformation campaigns as an example of where absolute ethical principles apply:

> "What they do is they purposefully take the discussion and make it more polarized and more violent... attacking individuals. And we don't understand that we play into that, and by the time we realize it was a foreign campaign, we've already lost each other; we're not speaking the same language."

He argued this is "absolutely wrong" — and that the ultra-liberal value of free speech should not be exploited to enable societal harm.

### The Ethics of Ian's NeuroKick Tool

When Ian described his AI leadership development tool (which listens to conversations to detect diminishing behaviors), Ori offered nuanced ethical guidance:

1. **Transparency**: Inform the team about the tool before using it
2. **Consent**: Seek their agreement to participate
3. **Sharing**: Share the results with them, not just use them privately
4. **Data ownership**: Consider whether employees should own the data stored about them
5. **Right to erasure**: Can someone request their data be deleted?

This aligned with Ian's own moral intuition — "the feeling that something about using the tool without my team's knowledge didn't sit right." Ori illuminated that ethical considerations must extend beyond functionality to encompass impact on human relationships and trust.

### Societal Values in Policy-Making

Different societies hold distinct values (e.g., diversity, pluralism, and kindness in Canada) that don't universally translate across borders. Ori argued that governance of technology should be guided by societal values expressed through policy-making, rather than strict adherence to traditional ethical doctrines.

The challenge: domestic digital regulations inevitably intersect with international norms, creating tensions in an "evolving landscape of ethics-turning-into-policy in a globally connected world."

### The Fluidity of Context in the Digital Age

In pre-digital times, the meaning of concepts shifted slowly. In the digital era, meaning shifts rapidly — social media's perceived role changed dramatically within a decade. "This shift underscores the fluid nature of context and its capacity to reshape our understanding and engagement with technology."

Real-time context is therefore critical for navigating the swift currents of meaning and values.

## Ethical Principles for Technology Development

Drawing from the conversation, key ethical principles for contextual intelligence include:

1. **Consent** — Users must know what data is being collected and agree to it
2. **Purpose** — Clear articulation of why data is being collected and how it's used
3. **Ownership** — Who owns the data? Can users access and control their own data?
4. **Right to erasure** — Can users request deletion of their data?
5. **Transparency** — Results and insights should be shared with those affected
6. **Dignity** — Technology must uphold human dignity as a non-negotiable value
7. **Societal responsibility** — Consider broader implications beyond immediate use case

## Relevance to Contextual Intelligence

Ori contributes the *moral dimension* of context. His work demonstrates that contextual intelligence is not just about understanding customer needs — it's about understanding the ethical landscape in which products operate. Products built without considering the ethical context of their users' lives risk not just market failure but genuine harm.

His framework for evaluating AI tools (consent, purpose, ownership, erasure, transparency) provides a practical ethical checklist that complements the [Customer Context Map](/frameworks/customer-context-map)'s focus on understanding customer needs.
