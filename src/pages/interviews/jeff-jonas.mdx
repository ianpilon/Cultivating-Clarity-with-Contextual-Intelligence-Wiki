# Jeff Jonas

**Summary**: Discovery interview with Jeff Jonas (October 6, 2023) — a pioneer of "engineered serendipity," former IBM chief scientist, and inventor of entity resolution technology. Jonas provided the foundational definition of context used throughout the book and offered critical feedback on Ian's context-centered design framework.

## Background

Jeff Jonas is a data science pioneer known for solving the "Entity Resolution Problem" — the challenge of determining when different data records refer to the same real-world entity. His career has been shaped by a single pivotal moment in the 1970s.

### The Origin Story — Bruce Brown and CompuServe
When Jeff was approximately 14, his mother (a lawyer) took him and a family friend named Bruce Brown (a carpenter-inventor) to see one of the earliest personal computers — possibly a TRS-80. The sales pitch was that the computer could "connect to other computers and you can search for anything in the world."

Bruce Brown, who was studying how to use copper pipes and airflow without electricity to create cooling (and believed he was the only person in the world working on it), typed his research topic into what was likely CompuServe. The results came back with approximately 100 hits of abstracts, including current research being conducted in Russia.

Jonas recalls: "I remember the look on Bruce's face, that was when I had this deep sudden realization, a profound moment... I didn't just think, when I grow up that I want to learn this. I just thought to myself... I do this." This was the moment he recognized that finding non-obvious connections in data was his calling.

### The Black Mold and Alzheimer's Story
Jonas met a researcher on a plane who studied a type of black mold that kills 30% of the world's crops. When he asked if she knew of anyone doing synergistic research studying similar proteins and molecules, she said no. Jonas recognized that there could be researchers studying similar protein patterns in Alzheimer's research — but without a system to connect these disparate data sets, the connection would never be made.

### Termite Mounds and Skyscrapers
Researchers in Africa studying termite mounds found that certain mound architectures are naturally climate-controlled. Skyscraper designers picked up on this insight for energy-efficient building design. Three university programs now study this intersection. Jonas points to this as "serendipity" — but argues that *engineered* serendipity would be an algorithm that makes such connections systematically.

## Key Concepts

### Engineered Serendipity
See full page: [Engineered Serendipity](/concepts/engineered-serendipity)

Jonas's signature concept. While serendipity is stumbling into non-obvious connections (like Bruce Brown finding Russian research on his niche topic), *engineered* serendipity is building algorithms that systematically connect diverse data that was "not intended to be combined." The explosion of digital data makes this increasingly possible — but the data is "not yet being connected."

> "If you could engineer connecting diverse data, not looking just at the single puzzle pieces in isolation, with algorithms, you're going to find things like the black mold and the Alzheimer's researchers and connect them in new and interesting ways; that's engineered serendipity!"

### The Definition of Context
Jonas provided the definition Ian used as his working framework for nearly a decade:

> "My go-to definition of context is to better understand something by taking into account the things around it."

He elaborated with the bat example: "If I say the word bat in a sentence, you're going to listen to all the other words I say next to understand what kind of bat I'm talking about." A single puzzle piece is ambiguous; as the puzzle takes form, meaning becomes "pinned down."

More formally: "Context is the process that allows you to turn diverse observations into a new perception or a perspective."

### The James Webb Telescope Metaphor — Team Alignment
When Jonas hired a new team member with very different views, his previously well-aligned team became dysfunctional — "like trying to herd bunnies." This coincided with the James Webb Space Telescope's mirror-tuning process. Just as the telescope needed macro and micro mirror alignment over months, Jonas needed to realign his team. He created a presentation called "The Essence of Senzing" to bring alignment back.

### Feedback on Ian's Context-Centered Design Framework
When Ian showed Jonas his visual diagram with "whole truth" at the center, Jonas immediately pushed back:

> "I think how you're using the notion of 'whole truth' at the center of the diagram is problematic. You could call that focal point an aligned perspective."

His reasoning: using "whole truth" invites endless expansion of the discussion as people challenge whose truth it is. Instead, the process should be:

1. **Align perspectives** — Bring forward observations (hard things, easy things, goals, desired outcomes)
2. **Align outcomes** — From shared perspectives, agree on what success looks like
3. **Align measures** — Only then define KPIs for measuring success

> "You stumble backwards, and you don't get to KPIs too early because you'll need to align on outcomes first."

Jonas also quoted F. Scott Fitzgerald: "The test of a first-rate intelligence is the ability to hold two opposing ideas in mind at the same time and still keep the ability to function." He warned against locking in on a single "truth" too early, as this kills the diversity of perspective needed for real intelligence.

### The Rubik's Cube of Business Design
Jonas compared business alignment to a Rubik's Cube — moving one element (getting the blue square in place) may foul another (yellow square falls off), potentially breaking something like customer support. Business design is a complex system where changes propagate unpredictably.

### Higher and Lower Level KPIs
A crucial insight for [Team Alignment and Context](/synthesis/team-alignment-and-context): higher-level KPIs must align with lower-level KPIs to ensure the system doesn't drive incompatible behavior. Misaligned metrics create misaligned teams.

## Relevance to Contextual Intelligence

Jonas contributes the *informational* dimension of context — how connecting data that wasn't meant to be combined can yield breakthrough insights. His work on entity resolution is essentially context resolution at a data level: determining that two records refer to the same entity by considering the information *around* each record.

His feedback on Ian's framework also introduced a crucial process insight: context-centric design is not about finding "the truth" but about systematically aligning perspectives toward shared outcomes. This pragmatic framing avoids philosophical quagmires while still honoring the importance of multiple viewpoints.
