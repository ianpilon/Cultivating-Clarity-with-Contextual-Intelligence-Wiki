# Coherence Constraints

**Summary**: Alexander Naumenko's linguistic concept explaining how questions shape and constrain understanding — a question is a declarative sentence missing one piece, and the rest of the question constrains what can fill the gap.

**Sources**: CULTIVATING CLARITY.md

**Last updated**: 2026-04-14

---

## The Concept

> "Consider any question; it's essentially a declarative sentence missing one piece, highlighted by the question word. The rest of the question constrains what can fill that gap. Not just anything fits." — Alexander Naumenko

Example: "How tall is that tree?" demands a measurement in response. The question constrains the answer space to numerical height values — you can't respond with a color, a location, or an emotion.

## Mechanism

Human intelligence uses questions as tools to fill knowledge gaps. Each question:
1. Identifies a specific gap (the question word)
2. Provides context that constrains valid answers (the rest of the sentence)
3. Narrows the possibility space through these constraints
4. Functions like a step in the 20 Questions process (see [Intelligence Through Differences](/concepts/intelligence-through-differences))

## Implications for Customer Research

The questions we ask in customer discovery constrain the answers we receive:
- Poorly framed questions (pablum layer) produce unconstrained, meaningless responses
- Well-framed questions (causal layer) constrain responses toward actionable insights
- Questions about behavior ("what happened?") constrain differently than questions about opinion ("what do you think?")
- [Bob Moesta](/interviews/bob-moesta)'s technique of deliberately wrong playback creates coherence constraints that force corrections — more constraining (and revealing) than open questions
