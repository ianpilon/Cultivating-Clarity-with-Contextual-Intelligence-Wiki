# Be Goals and Do Goals

**Summary**: The distinction between immediate action-oriented objectives (Do Goals) and long-term identity aspirations (Be Goals) — a key organizing principle in the Customer Context Map that determines which level of context resolution to operate at.

**Sources**: CULTIVATING CLARITY.md

**Last updated**: 2026-04-14

---

## The Distinction

### Do Goals (Low–Medium Resolution)
Immediate, action-oriented results the customer wants to achieve right now or in the near term.

**Examples**:
- "I need to find running shoes that prevent injury"
- "I need to complete this checkout process"
- "I need to prepare for my team meeting tomorrow"

Do Goals operate primarily in [System 1 and early System 2](/concepts/system-1-and-system-2-thinking) thinking — they're concrete, time-bounded, and directly tied to specific actions or tasks.

### Be Goals (High Resolution)
Long-term, aspirational outcomes — who the customer is trying to *become*. These are identity-level goals that transcend any single product interaction.

**Examples**:
- "I want to be the kind of person who completes a marathon"
- "I want to be a better leader for my team"
- "I want to be someone who makes a meaningful impact"

Be Goals operate in deep [System 2](/concepts/system-1-and-system-2-thinking) thinking — they're abstract, open-ended, and connected to values, identity, and life narrative.

## The Connection

Do Goals are the stepping stones toward Be Goals. A customer's immediate action ("buy running shoes") serves a short-term objective ("prevent injury during training") which serves a long-term aspiration ("become someone who completes a marathon" → "prove my resilience after illness").

Understanding this chain — from Do to Be — is the essence of moving up the [Context Resolution Gradient](/concepts/context-resolution-gradient):

| Level | Goal Type | Question |
|-------|----------|----------|
| Low resolution | Micro Do Goals | "What are they clicking on?" |
| Medium resolution | Macro Do Goals | "What progress are they trying to make?" |
| High resolution | Be Goals | "Who are they trying to become?" |

## Role in the Customer Context Map

The [Customer Context Map](/frameworks/customer-context-map) starts with identifying both goal types:

1. **Identify Do Goals** through behavioral data and interviews about recent actions
2. **Identify Be Goals** through deep ethnographic research and life-narrative interviews
3. **Map the connection** between them to understand the full context of customer behavior

Products that serve only Do Goals have limited value and are easily replaced. Products that connect to Be Goals become meaningful partners in the customer's life journey — creating the kind of deep engagement described throughout the book.

## Connection to Jobs to Be Done

[Bob Moesta](/interviews/bob-moesta)'s output vs. outcome distinction maps directly:
- **Output** ≈ Do Goal (the measurable result: lost weight, bought shoes, completed task)
- **Outcome** ≈ Be Goal (the meaningful life change: ski with kids, prove resilience, become a leader)

The [Jobs to Be Done](/concepts/jobs-to-be-done) framework investigates both — but the deepest insights come from understanding the Be Goals that give Do Goals their meaning.

## Michael Levin's Perspective

The [Cognitive Light Cone](/concepts/cognitive-light-cone) concept provides a biological analogy: organisms with small cognitive light cones pursue only immediate goals (a cell managing sugar levels ≈ Do Goals). Organisms with large cognitive light cones pursue goals that transcend their lifespan (humans working toward world peace ≈ Be Goals). The uniquely human capacity for Be Goals is what makes high-resolution customer understanding both possible and necessary.
