# Observer-Dependent Context

**Summary**: Michael Levin's principle that context is not an objective property of the environment but is constructed by each observer based on their perspective, capabilities, goals, and history. What counts as context depends on who is looking.

**Sources**: CULTIVATING CLARITY.md

**Last updated**: 2026-04-14

---

## The Principle

> "Context is in the eye of the beholder. It is a best guess. It is a set of affordances where you look around and you say, well, I can sit on this, and I can eat this other thing, and I can hide under this other thing. Or, at some point, I can have a deep conversation with this other thing." — Michael Levin

Every agent constructs its own version of what the environment means based on:
- What it can perceive (sensory capabilities)
- What it needs (current goals, see [Cognitive Light Cone](/concepts/cognitive-light-cone))
- What it has learned (past experience, memory)
- What it can do (available actions and affordances)

"If you're good at it, you'll have a very great adaptive life. And if you're not good at it, then you'll leave a lot on the table."

## Cross-Perspective Validation

### Nick Shackleton-Jones
Different entities encode context differently: engineers use measurements, computers use light patterns, humans use emotional reactions. A tree is "something that would total my car" to a human driver, but just a pattern of light to a self-driving car's camera.

### Alexander Naumenko
"We don't consider all properties of an object at once; it depends on the context we're in. The same apple can be seen as food or as a makeshift weapon." Which properties are salient depends entirely on the observer's current needs and goals.

### Bob Moesta
The steak vs. pizza example: the "value" of each food depends entirely on the observer's current situation — rushing with kids (pizza gains value) or celebrating (steak gains value). The food itself hasn't changed; the observer's context has.

## Implications for Customer Discovery

If context is observer-dependent, then:

1. **You cannot assume your context matches the customer's**: What product teams see as valuable may be irrelevant from the customer's perspective
2. **Multiple customers see different contexts**: The same product creates different affordances for different users
3. **Context changes over time**: As customers' goals, needs, and situations shift, their context shifts — and the product's meaning shifts with it
4. **You must discover, not assume**: There is no shortcut to understanding how customers construct their context — it requires direct engagement ([Empathy in Innovation](/concepts/empathy-in-innovation))
