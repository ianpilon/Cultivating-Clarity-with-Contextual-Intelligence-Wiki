# Jobs to Be Done

**Summary**: The Jobs to Be Done (JTBD) framework, co-created by Bob Moesta, provides a methodology for understanding why customers make choices by focusing on the "progress" they're trying to make in their lives rather than the products they buy. It is the most actionable framework for applying contextual intelligence to product development.

**Sources**: CULTIVATING CLARITY.md

**Last updated**: 2026-04-14

---

## Core Principle

People don't buy products — they "hire" products to help them make progress in their lives. The "job" is not about the product's features but about the outcome the customer is trying to achieve in a specific context.

The framework shifts the question from "What features should we build?" to "What progress is the customer trying to make, and what is getting in their way?"

## The Forces of Progress

Four forces act on every customer decision:

### Forces that promote change:
1. **Push** — Dissatisfaction with the current situation ("My computer is too slow")
2. **Pull** — Attraction toward a new solution ("That new laptop looks fast and sleek")

### Forces that resist change:
3. **Habit** — Comfort with the known ("I know how this computer works")
4. **Anxiety** — Fear of the unknown ("What if the new one is worse? What if I lose my files?")

For a customer to switch, the combined push + pull must exceed habit + anxiety. This is why "[bitchin' ain't switching](/concepts/the-switching-moment)" — complaining (push alone) without sufficient pull and insufficient anxiety reduction doesn't produce action.

## Output vs. Outcome

A critical distinction from [Bob Moesta](/interviews/bob-moesta):

- **Output**: The measurable result (lost 100 pounds)
- **Outcome**: The meaningful life change (can ski with adult kids)

The same output can serve completely different outcomes for different people. Product teams must discover outcomes, not just outputs. "Your questioning strategy focuses on outcomes, bypassing the standard outputs."

## Demand Exists Independent of Supply

The [Demand Side vs Supply Side](/concepts/demand-side-vs-supply-side) principle: people have needs regardless of whether a product exists to meet them. The question "How many people want to go back to school but can't?" revealed massive latent demand that had nothing to do with any existing product. Southern New Hampshire University grew from 2,500 students to the largest university in the world by discovering and serving this demand.

## The Three Dimensions of a Job

Every job has three dimensions (see [Functional Social Emotional Dimensions](/concepts/functional-social-emotional-dimensions)):

1. **Functional** — What the product physically does ("My car gets me to work")
2. **Social** — How others perceive the choice ("My boss will think I'm competent")
3. **Emotional** — How it makes the person feel ("I feel safe and confident")

These dimensions transform between states like energy: a functional problem (slow computer) creates social anxiety (boss's perception) which builds emotional intensity (fear of job loss) until the accumulated energy triggers a behavior change.

## Integration with the Customer Context Map

JTBD operates at the medium and high levels of the [Context Resolution Gradient](/concepts/context-resolution-gradient):

- **Low resolution** (System 1): Captures immediate behaviors but not the "why"
- **Medium resolution** (System 1→2): Reveals patterns, goals, and the job being done
- **High resolution** (System 2): Uncovers the life narrative — the "Be Goals" (who the customer is trying to become)

The [Customer Context Map](/frameworks/customer-context-map) integrates JTBD with [Information Foraging Theory](/concepts/information-foraging-theory) and [System 1 and System 2 Thinking](/concepts/system-1-and-system-2-thinking) to create a comprehensive framework for customer discovery at all resolution levels.

## Interview Technique: Investigating the Timeline

JTBD interviews focus on the timeline of a decision — specifically the [switching moment](/concepts/the-switching-moment):

1. **First thought** — When did you first think about changing?
2. **Passive looking** — When did you start noticing alternatives?
3. **Active looking** — When did you start seriously researching?
4. **Decision event** — What tipped you over the edge?
5. **Post-purchase** — How do you feel about the decision now?

At each stage, the interviewer probes for the push, pull, habit, and anxiety forces in play, uncovering the [causal layer](/concepts/three-layers-of-language) beneath surface-level explanations.
