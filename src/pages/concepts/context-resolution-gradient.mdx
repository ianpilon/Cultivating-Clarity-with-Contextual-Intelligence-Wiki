# Context Resolution Gradient

**Summary**: The framework for understanding customer context as a spectrum from concrete micro-interactions (low resolution) to abstract life narratives (high resolution), with each level demanding different thinking modes, research methods, and design responses. Uses Claude Shannon images at different pixel densities as a mnemonic.

**Sources**: CULTIVATING CLARITY.md

**Last updated**: 2026-04-14

---

## The Core Metaphor

Inspired by Claude Shannon's work on information theory, the book uses images of Shannon at three resolutions as an analogy for understanding customer context:

- **2 pixels** → Low resolution: only the most basic outline is visible
- **8 pixels** → Medium resolution: basic features become discernible
- **High resolution** → Full detail: the fine nuances of the person are clear

This visual metaphor serves as a "memory retrieval mechanism" — practitioners can picture these images to quickly orient themselves on the context gradient and determine what level of understanding they're operating at.

## The Three Levels

### Low Context Resolution — Immediate Interactions

**Focus**: The customer's immediate, instinctive interactions with a product or service

**Thinking mode**: [System 1](/concepts/system-1-and-system-2-thinking) — fast, automatic, subconscious

**Customer behavior**: Information foraging — quickly scanning for relevant cues and acting without deep reflection. Limited attention spans, preference for familiarity, impulse-driven decisions.

**What you capture**: Clicks, views, purchases, time-on-page, bounce rates — the "what" of behavior

**What you miss**: The "why" behind those behaviors

**Research methods**: Click-through rates, heat maps, A/B testing, purchase history analysis, instantaneous reaction studies, recognition-based judgment tests

**Design implications**: Simple, intuitive interfaces; consistent with conventions; prominent calls-to-action; emotionally resonant copy; minimal friction in common paths

**Example**: A customer clicks on a running shoe ad. You know something caught their attention, but not whether they're genuinely interested, just curious, or clicked by accident.

### Medium Context Resolution — Patterns and Goals

**Focus**: The broader patterns and contexts that shape customer behavior over time

**Thinking mode**: System 1→2 transition — deliberate, reflective, goal-oriented

**Customer behavior**: Sensemaking — actively questioning assumptions, seeking information, constructing narratives about their experience in terms of broader goals

**What you capture**: Decision-making processes, trade-off evaluations, goal hierarchies, the "jobs" customers are hiring products to do

**What you miss**: The deepest values and life context that give goals meaning

**Research methods**: Customer interviews, problem-solving studies, adaptability research, informed choice experiments, [Jobs to Be Done](/concepts/jobs-to-be-done) timeline investigations

**Design implications**: Support deliberate decision-making; provide comparison tools; align marketing with customer goals; anticipate pain points in the customer journey

**Example**: Through interviews, you learn that many running shoe buyers are training for marathons, concerned about injury prevention, or seeking environmentally sustainable options. Patterns emerge that reveal the "jobs" being done.

### High Context Resolution — The Life Narrative

**Focus**: The deep, often unspoken desires and aspirations that shape the course of a customer's life

**Thinking mode**: Deep [System 2](/concepts/system-1-and-system-2-thinking) — reflective, integrative, connecting disparate behaviors into a holistic view

**Customer behavior**: Identity construction — making choices that express values, mark life transitions, and advance toward becoming who they want to be

**What you capture**: Values, aspirations, life narratives, "Be Goals" (who the customer is trying to become), the emotional and symbolic significance of choices

**Research methods**: Strategic planning studies, values-driven decision experiments, life transition research, deep ethnographic engagement

**Design implications**: Become a partner in the customer's life journey; create content and communities around shared aspirations; design for emotional and symbolic resonance, not just functionality

**Example**: The marathon runner is approaching a significant birthday, sees the race as marking a new life chapter, and is motivated by a desire to prove resilience after a health scare. Understanding this transforms the product from "shoes" to "a partner in personal transformation."

## The Interconnectedness of Levels

These levels are not isolated but mutually informing:

- **Bottom-up**: Low-resolution insights (patterns in click data) provide clues about broader goals (medium) and life narratives (high)
- **Top-down**: High-resolution understanding (life aspirations) helps interpret micro-behaviors (why they clicked *that* specific ad)
- **Iterative**: The research process itself moves through levels — starting with tactical observation, building to strategic understanding, and cycling back to validate

> "The key insight about customer contextual intelligence gathering is that the level of context resolution we choose dictates the mode of thinking we employ, and vice versa."

## The Research Journey

1. **Tactical research** → Explore and authenticate solutions for well-defined problems (low)
2. **Operational research** → Quantify effectiveness and optimize existing solutions (low→medium)
3. **Strategic research** → Identify new opportunities and unaddressed needs (medium→high)
4. **Exploratory research** → Venture into new markets and potential growth areas (high)
