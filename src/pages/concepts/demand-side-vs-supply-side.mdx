# Demand-Side vs. Supply-Side Thinking

**Summary**: Bob Moesta's principle that demand exists independent of supply — customer needs and struggles exist regardless of whether products exist to address them. Innovation should start from demand (customer progress) not supply (what we can build).

**Sources**: CULTIVATING CLARITY.md

**Last updated**: 2026-04-14

---

## The Principle

Most product teams operate from the supply side: "What can we build? What features can we add? What technology can we leverage?" This is backwards.

The demand side asks: "What progress are people trying to make? What struggles do they face? What would they pay to have resolved?" Demand exists whether or not a solution has been built.

## The SNHU Case Study

**Southern New Hampshire University (2010)**:
- Started with 2,500 students, 50 in remote learning
- President Paul LeBlanc asked: "How many people want to go back to school but can't?"
- Discovered massive latent demand from people hindered by traditional campus requirements
- Reimagined the system for online learners
- Became the largest university in the world
- Extended campus to Africa
- Reduced cost of college education by 70%

> "All because he thought of outcomes first." (source: CULTIVATING CLARITY.md)

The university didn't create new demand — it discovered *existing* demand that was invisible from a supply-side perspective.

## The Transistor Radio Parallel

Moesta uses the transistor radio as an example of innovation that was "better than nothing." Before portable radios, teenagers had no way to listen to music independently — they shared the family radio in the living room. The transistor radio wasn't technically superior to home systems, but it fulfilled a completely unmet demand by creating a new category.

## Innovation From Customers, Not Engineers

> "I used to think innovation came from scientists and engineers. The real lesson I learned was that innovation comes from the customers."

This doesn't mean customers design solutions — it means the *signal* for what to build comes from understanding customer struggles, not from technical capability.

## Implications

1. **Validate demand before building supply**: The [Customer Context Map](/frameworks/customer-context-map) starts with understanding customer goals and struggles, not product features
2. **Look for non-consumption**: The biggest opportunities often lie with people who *aren't* using any solution — latent demand
3. **Distinguish outputs from outcomes**: Supply produces outputs; demand seeks outcomes (see [Bob Moesta](/interviews/bob-moesta))
4. **Customer vs. consumer**: The buyer (customer) and the user (consumer) may have different demands that must both be satisfied
