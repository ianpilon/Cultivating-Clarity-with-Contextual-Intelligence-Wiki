# Affective Context

**Summary**: The Affective Context Model, proposed by Nick Shackleton-Jones, asserts that learning is the process by which people attach emotional (affective) sense to information. Context is encoded through emotional reactions — what we care about — not through data or logic. This concept is a recurring theme across multiple interviews in the book.

**Sources**: CULTIVATING CLARITY.md

**Last updated**: 2026-04-14

---

## The Model

The Affective Context Model proposes that:

1. **Learning is fundamentally emotional**: Information gains true significance only when framed within an affective context
2. **Memory is emotion-dependent**: We remember what we cared about — moments of joy, curiosity, fear, or discomfort persist while neutral information fades
3. **Human sense-making is rooted in evolved mechanisms**: Our cognitive processes evolved under different evolutionary pressures than we experience today, meaning rational thought is "but a fraction of our cognitive capacity"
4. **Context is encoded through reaction**: Humans don't measure their environment (like engineers) or pattern-match it (like computers) — we react to it emotionally, and those reactions form our contextual understanding

## The Forgetting Curve Connection

Hermann Ebbinghaus's forgetting curve demonstrates that information learned through rote memorization decays rapidly. The Affective Context Model explains *why*: rote memorization provides no emotional anchor. Information presented without affective resonance is, cognitively speaking, *meaningless* — the brain has no reason to retain it.

Contrast this with emotionally charged experiences: choosing a college, buying a first house, witnessing a crisis. These memories persist for decades because dopamine, serotonin, and cortisol "amplify and prioritize" them (as [Bob Moesta](/interviews/bob-moesta) explains).

## Cross-Interview Validation

The affective dimension of context is validated across multiple interviews:

### Nick Shackleton-Jones (originator)
Humans encode context through emotional reactions. A tree matters to a driver because crashing would total the car. A tiger on the road creates a memory that dominates your entire month. "A computer will say, Tiger, not interesting, and move on."

### Bob Moesta
"If there's any emotion, people remember events like choosing a college or buying a house because dopamine, serotonin, and cortisol amplify and prioritize those memories." The [switching moment](/concepts/the-switching-moment) is memorable precisely because it's emotionally charged.

### Alexander Naumenko
"Emotionally rich memories are stored faster and for longer periods." Concepts discussed together form tighter memory relationships "especially when emotions are involved." Rehearsal imposes our emotional point of view on observations.

### Michael Levin
Memory is a message from your past self to your future self. What gets encoded is not raw data but generalized, contextually relevant information shaped by the organism's current needs and goals — which are inherently tied to survival and wellbeing (emotional stakes).

### Mark Rubin
Inspiration — an emotional state — has "the most leverage" of any motivational force. People will trade their time and labor for the *feeling* of inspiration, as demonstrated by pilgrims walking for six weeks between churches.

## Implications for Product Development

### Experience Specifications > Functional Specifications
A product can meet every functional requirement and still fail if it doesn't engage users emotionally. The learning management system example: functionally excellent, experientially dead. "Was it fun? Did I enjoy it? Did it solve my problem right here, right now?"

### Designing for Emotional Resonance
Products must create moments of affective salience — moments where users *care* about the interaction. This doesn't mean manipulating emotions but rather understanding what users genuinely care about and aligning the product experience with those emotional realities.

### The Siemens Experiment Warning
Nick's experiment at Siemens showed that adding cognitive psychology theory, interaction design, multiple encoding levels, and narratives to learning content performed *worse* than plain text. The implication: adding complexity without adding emotional relevance is counterproductive. Affective context cannot be artificially bolted on — it must emerge from genuine alignment with what users care about.

### The Tiger vs. the Tiger Slide
If you saw a real tiger while driving, you'd remember it for months. If you saw a PowerPoint slide with the word "tiger," you'd forget it by lunch. Same information, radically different affective context. Products that operate at the "PowerPoint slide" level of emotional engagement will be forgotten. Products that create "tiger on the road" moments will be remembered and valued.

## Connection to System 1 and System 2 Thinking

Affective context operates primarily in [System 1](/concepts/system-1-and-system-2-thinking) — the fast, intuitive, emotional processing that happens before conscious deliberation. However, its effects persist into System 2 by shaping which information we attend to, remember, and prioritize during deliberate analysis.

The [Customer Context Map](/frameworks/customer-context-map) incorporates this by starting at low [context resolution](/concepts/context-resolution-gradient) (capturing immediate emotional reactions) and building toward high resolution (understanding the life narrative that gives those emotions meaning).

## The Machine Ethics Problem

If affective context is how humans process ethics ("there are no rules for ethics, because ethics is based on how you feel"), then machines without genuine emotional capacity cannot be truly ethical. Current AI systems can only simulate care through statistical patterns in training data. This creates a fundamental gap between human and machine contextual intelligence that has profound implications for AI governance (see [Ethics and Context](/synthesis/ethics-and-context)).
