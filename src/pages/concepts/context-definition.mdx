# Context — Definitions Across Perspectives

**Summary**: A compilation of how each of the nine interviewees and the author define and conceptualize "context," revealing a rich, multi-dimensional understanding that no single definition can capture.

**Sources**: CULTIVATING CLARITY.md

**Last updated**: 2026-04-14

---

## The Challenge of Defining Context

One of the book's implicit discoveries is that context itself resists a singular definition. Each expert brought a different framing, and the synthesis of these framings produces a richer understanding than any one alone. The word "context" appears hundreds of times throughout the book, yet its meaning subtly shifts depending on who is speaking and what domain they operate in.

## Definitions by Perspective

### Jeff Jonas — The Foundational Definition
> "To better understand something by taking into account the things around it."

This became Ian's working definition for nearly a decade. Jonas elaborated with the puzzle piece metaphor: a single puzzle piece in isolation is ambiguous, but as pieces connect, meaning becomes "pinned down." Context is the agglomeration of observations that narrows interpretation.

He further refined: "Context is the process that allows you to turn diverse observations into a new perception or a perspective." (source: CULTIVATING CLARITY.md)

### Nick Shackleton-Jones — The Affective Definition
Context, for Shackleton-Jones, is fundamentally *emotional*. Humans don't encode their environment through measurements (like engineers) or patterns of light (like computers). We encode through emotional reactions — what we *care* about. A tree matters to a driver because crashing into it would total the car. A cat matters because we care about cats. Context is codified according to our reaction to it.

This is the [Affective Context Model](/concepts/affective-context): meaning is inseparable from feeling. (source: CULTIVATING CLARITY.md)

### Alexander Naumenko — The Linguistic Definition
> "For me, context is all the objects that are available to the speaker and to the listener, and relationships among those objects."

Language filters our focus through the properties of objects when we communicate. References in language are "nets" not "harpoons" — they capture all fitting descriptions. "When we stack those nets like filters, what passes through is the meaning. So meaning depends always on context, always." (source: CULTIVATING CLARITY.md)

### Bob Moesta — The Situational Definition
For Moesta, context is the *situation* that determines value. His steak-vs-pizza example is definitive: people say they like both, but the *context* of eating them differs completely. Pizza is grabbed when rushing with kids before bedtime. Steak is a celebration with wine and company. The same person, the same preferences, but context "can significantly enhance or diminish the value of a product."

Context is what makes the "causal layer" of language visible — the actual sequence of events and motivations behind choices. (source: CULTIVATING CLARITY.md)

### Michael Levin — The Biological Definition
> "Context is everything."

For Levin, context is observer-dependent and dynamic. Memory itself is context-dependent — the caterpillar's memory of "leaves on a colored disc" must be generalized to "food in that location" for the butterfly, because the butterfly doesn't eat leaves. The memory only makes sense when remapped to the *current* context.

"Context is in the eye of the beholder. It is a best guess. It is a set of affordances where you look around and you say, well, I can sit on this, and I can eat this other thing, and I can hide under this other thing." Every agent must build their own categories of how to "cut up the world into functional pieces." (source: CULTIVATING CLARITY.md)

### Robert Scoble — The Technological Definition
Scoble's "Age of Context" frames context as the data layer that makes technology personally relevant. Google Maps understands that your experience differs when entering a shopping store versus a sports arena. Context is what enables technology to adapt to the user's current situation. "Humans are patterns, and we're studying those patterns." (source: CULTIVATING CLARITY.md)

### Ori Freiman — The Ethical Definition
> "Context is a very important factor in influencing meaning, but also interpreting meaning of anything that has to do with ethics, moral values, and with the principles we adopt, and the judgments that we have."

Context shapes not just product value but the very meaning of ethical principles. What counts as "right" shifts with technological, cultural, and temporal context. Social media went from being lauded for democracy to being scrutinized for societal harm — same technology, shifted context. (source: CULTIVATING CLARITY.md)

### Mark Rubin — The Systems Definition
For Rubin, context emerges from the cross-functional connections between mental models. Everything he knows is "cross-functionally connected in different ways that I can navigate." Context is the network of relationships between concepts — position, velocity, acceleration, momentum, friction — understood not as isolated variables but as an interconnected system. (source: CULTIVATING CLARITY.md)

### Amjad Hassoun — The Computational Definition
In the AI/LLM context, context is what the Transformer's attention mechanism processes. It examines "all the context and data it has been trained on" and, as it generates output, "considers the relevant context and notes to produce the best output." Context windows, RAG systems, and prompt engineering are all attempts to provide machines with something resembling contextual understanding. (source: CULTIVATING CLARITY.md)

### Ian Pilon — The Synthesized Definition
Ian's definition evolved through the book. He moved from Jonas's foundational framing to a richer understanding that context is a *gradient* — ranging from concrete micro-interactions to abstract life narratives. Context is dynamic, emotional, observer-dependent, culturally situated, ethically loaded, and biologically fundamental. It is the lens through which all meaning is constructed.

## A Possible Unifying Frame

While no single definition captures all perspectives, a synthesis might read:

**Context is the full constellation of factors — situational, emotional, psychological, social, cultural, temporal, and biological — that an observer brings to bear when interpreting meaning, making decisions, and pursuing goals. It is not fixed but dynamic, not objective but observer-dependent, and not peripheral but foundational to all understanding.**
