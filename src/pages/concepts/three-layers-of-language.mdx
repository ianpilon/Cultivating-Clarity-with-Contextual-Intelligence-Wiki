# Three Layers of Language

**Summary**: Bob Moesta's framework describing three levels at which human communication operates — pablum (surface platitudes), fantasy/nightmare (exaggerated ideals or fears), and causal (the actual sequence of events). Only the causal layer reveals truth about customer motivations, and most product teams never reach it.

**Sources**: CULTIVATING CLARITY.md

**Last updated**: 2026-04-14

---

## The Three Layers

### Layer 1: Pablum
Surface-level conversation where precision is lacking and meaningful engagement is rare.

**Example**: "How was your day?" → "It was good."

What does "good" mean? Almost nothing. This layer is social lubricant, not information. Bob finds it "absurd" that LLMs are being trained primarily on this layer of language — the shallowest, least meaningful form of human communication.

**In customer research**: "Do you like our product?" → "Yeah, it's fine." This tells you nothing about what the customer actually experiences, needs, or struggles with.

### Layer 2: Fantasy / Nightmare
Where things are exaggerated, either as better or worse than they actually are. This is the layer of aspiration and fear — "almost like a commercial."

**Example**: "I want a car that makes me feel free and powerful!" (fantasy) or "Without a reliable car, my whole life would fall apart!" (nightmare)

**In customer research**: This is the layer customers operate in when you ask what they *want*. Their answers reflect idealized or catastrophized versions of reality, not the actual experience. It's why Henry Ford's apocryphal quote is misused — customers expressing desires at the fantasy layer would say "faster horses" because they're describing an idealized version of what they already know.

### Layer 3: Causal
The deepest layer — understanding the actual sequence of events, cause and effect.

> "That's where you find the truth." — Bob Moesta

**Example**: "Tell me about the last time you bought a car. What was happening in your life? What triggered the thought? What did you try first? What didn't work?"

**In customer research**: This layer requires investigating the timeline of decisions and actions. It's uncovered not by asking what people want but by asking what happened — in sequence, with contextual detail. The [Jobs to Be Done](/concepts/jobs-to-be-done) methodology is specifically designed to reach this layer.

## Why Most Research Stays Shallow

Product teams typically operate at layers 1 and 2:
- **Surveys**: "Rate your satisfaction 1-5" → pablum
- **Focus groups**: "What features would you like?" → fantasy
- **Customer advisory boards**: "What do you think of our roadmap?" → fantasy/pablum

Reaching the causal layer requires:
- One-on-one interviews (not group settings)
- Timeline-based questioning (what happened first, then what, then what)
- Focus on actual behavior rather than stated preferences
- Tolerance for "looking stupid" by asking simple, probing questions
- The technique of playing things back incorrectly to provoke corrections

## Connection to Other Concepts

### Bob Moesta's Interview Technique
Bob deliberately plays things back *incorrectly* to reach the causal layer: "I play things back incorrectly on purpose." When you state something wrong, people's natural instinct is to correct you — and the correction reveals deeper truth than the original statement.

> "No is way better than yes" — a "no" with a correction gives you the causal layer; a "yes" might just be pablum agreement.

### Affective Context
The causal layer connects to [Affective Context](/concepts/affective-context) because it's where emotional truth lives. When someone describes the actual sequence of events that led to a decision, the emotions embedded in those moments surface naturally — unlike the performed emotions of the fantasy layer.

### The Steak vs. Pizza Example
At the pablum layer: "I like steak and pizza."
At the fantasy layer: "I prefer steak — it's classy."
At the causal layer: "Last Thursday I grabbed pizza because I was running late and the kids needed to eat before bedtime. Last Saturday we went to a steakhouse to celebrate my promotion."

The causal layer reveals that the "preference" has nothing to do with the food and everything to do with the *context*.

## Implications for LLMs and AI

Bob's observation that LLMs are trained primarily on pablum-layer language raises questions about the depth of understanding these systems can achieve. If the causal layer — where truth lives — is underrepresented in training data, AI systems may be fundamentally limited in their ability to understand customer motivations.

This connects to [Amjad Hassoun](/interviews/amjad-hassoun)'s observation about the layers of processing needed for "a good, human-friendly response" — multiple prompt layers, safety filters, and cultural tuning sit on top of the base model, but the underlying training data remains predominantly shallow.
