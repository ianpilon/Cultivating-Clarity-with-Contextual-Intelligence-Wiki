# Customer Context Map

**Summary**: The primary actionable tool introduced in Cultivating Clarity — a visual framework that operationalizes contextual intelligence gathering by mapping customer context across three resolution levels, integrating System 1/System 2 thinking, Information Foraging Theory, and Jobs to Be Done into a tangible process for product innovators.

**Sources**: CULTIVATING CLARITY.md

**Last updated**: 2026-04-14

---

## Overview

The Customer Context Map is a visual diagram and framework designed to help product managers, UX designers, software engineers, and other innovators develop a rich, nuanced understanding of their customers. It is available online at: https://ianpilon.github.io/customercontextmap

The map combines three theoretical foundations:
1. **[System 1 and System 2 Thinking](/concepts/system-1-and-system-2-thinking)** — How customers (and teams) process information
2. **[Information Foraging Theory](/concepts/information-foraging-theory)** — How customers scan environments for relevant cues
3. **[Jobs to Be Done](/concepts/jobs-to-be-done)** — Why customers make the choices they do

These are organized across the [Context Resolution Gradient](/concepts/context-resolution-gradient) (low → medium → high resolution) to create a comprehensive framework for customer discovery.

## Purpose

The map serves as:
- A **catalyst for shifting** from System 1 to System 2 thinking about customers
- A **visual memory aid** (using Claude Shannon images at different resolutions)
- A **structured process** for moving from surface-level observations to deep customer understanding
- A **team alignment tool** for getting product, design, and engineering teams onto the same page about customer needs
- A **decision framework** for prioritizing what to build based on real customer struggles

## Step-by-Step Implementation

### Step 1: Identify Customer Goals

Two types of goals operate at different [resolution levels](/concepts/context-resolution-gradient):

- **Do Goals** (low-medium resolution): Immediate, action-oriented results the customer wants to achieve right now
- **Be Goals** (high resolution): Long-term, aspirational outcomes — who the customer is trying to *become*

Methods: interviews, surveys, observations. Remember: understanding the "why" behind goals is as important as the "what."

### Step 2: Map the Customer Journey

Create a visual map that highlights:
- Key interactions with the product/service
- [Struggling Moments](/concepts/struggling-moments) — where friction, frustration, or blockage occurs
- Points of success — where progress is made
- Emotional peaks and valleys throughout the journey

### Step 3: Determine Context Resolution Level

Choose the appropriate resolution based on objectives:
- **Low**: Understanding immediate usability and micro-interactions
- **Medium**: Understanding patterns, decision-making, and goal pursuit
- **High**: Understanding life narrative, values, and deep aspirations

Gather data accordingly — each level demands different methods (see [Research Methods by Resolution](/frameworks/research-methods-by-resolution)).

### Step 4: Apply Thinking Systems

Analyze how customers make decisions at the target resolution:
- **System 1 decisions**: Quick, subconscious reactions → design for intuition and ease
- **System 2 decisions**: Slow, deliberate processing → design for information richness and comparison

Tailor solutions to match the decision-making process involved.

### Step 5: Analyze Business Considerations

Evaluate two categories:
- **What you can control (Supply)**: Product features, pricing, distribution, messaging
- **What you cannot control (Demand)**: Customer needs, goals, [Struggling Moments](/concepts/struggling-moments), market conditions

Align offerings with customer needs; ensure solutions adapt to market demands. Remember [Bob Moesta](/interviews/bob-moesta)'s principle: [demand exists independent of supply](/concepts/demand-side-vs-supply-side).

### Step 6: Conduct Appropriate Research

Match research type to resolution level and business need:

| Research Type | Purpose | Resolution |
|--------------|---------|-----------|
| **Tactical** | Improve and optimize existing solutions | Low |
| **Operational** | Monitor and upgrade recent developments | Low→Medium |
| **Strategic** | Explore new opportunities within known problem spaces | Medium→High |
| **Exploratory** | Investigate new markets and potential growth areas | High |

## Integration with Jobs to Be Done Flow Diagram

The Customer Context Map works in conjunction with a [JTBD Flow Diagram](/frameworks/jobs-to-be-done-flow-diagram) that operationalizes the discovery process:

1. Low-resolution insights form the **starting point** — triggering curiosity about deeper patterns
2. Medium-resolution investigation reveals the **jobs** customers are hiring products for
3. High-resolution understanding uncovers the **life context** that gives those jobs meaning
4. This understanding shapes the **product backlog** — prioritizing features and experiences that address real [Struggling Moments](/concepts/struggling-moments)

## The Tangible Benefits

By implementing the Customer Context Map, teams can:

1. **Clearly define their target audience** — tailoring outreach to those with genuine need
2. **Focus research** — orienting around human needs rather than abstract market data
3. **Identify the right problems** — the ones people will pay to solve
4. **Gather regular feedback** — building loops that track evolving customer contexts
5. **Navigate timing and market readiness** — understanding broader conditions that affect adoption

## Implementation Principles

- **Start small**: Begin with a specific customer segment or single product
- **Iterate**: Continuously refine understanding through repeated cycles
- **Scale**: Expand to other segments once comfortable with the framework
- **Communicate**: Share findings across the team
- **Adapt**: Adjust approach based on new insights

## FitLife Example

A hypothetical fitness app (FitLife) used the Customer Context Map to discover that users were struggling with onboarding and dropping off before establishing workout routines. By understanding the context (new users' anxiety, information overload, lack of personalization), they refined the onboarding experience with personalized recommendations and progress tracking, resulting in significant increases in engagement and retention.
