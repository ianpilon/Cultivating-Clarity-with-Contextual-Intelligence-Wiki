# Team Alignment and Context

**Summary**: How shared contextual understanding accelerates team collaboration — drawing on Jeff Jonas's alignment framework, Bob Moesta's productive argument principles, Michael Levin's collective intelligence insights, and Ian's firsthand experience with misaligned product teams.

**Sources**: CULTIVATING CLARITY.md

**Last updated**: 2026-04-14

---

## The Problem

Ian observed a recurring pattern across companies: teams struggle to find common ground because each member brings a different contextual perspective. Product managers see market opportunity, designers see user experience, engineers see technical feasibility — and without shared context, opinion battles waste time and energy.

> "We're all experts at doing this type of thinking independently but as soon as we try to learn or build software with others, this common challenge of misalignment pops up."

## Jeff Jonas's Alignment Framework

When Jonas introduced a new team member with different views, his previously well-aligned team became dysfunctional ("herding bunnies"). His solution, inspired by the James Webb Space Telescope's mirror alignment:

### The Three-Step Process
1. **Align perspectives** — Each person brings forward observations (hard things, easy things, goals, desired outcomes)
2. **Align outcomes** — From shared perspectives, agree on what success looks like
3. **Align measures** — Only then define KPIs

> "You stumble backwards, and you don't get to KPIs too early."

### "Aligned Perspective" Not "Whole Truth"
Jonas rejected Ian's use of "whole truth" at the center of the alignment diagram. "Whole truth" invites endless debate about whose truth it is. "Aligned perspective" acknowledges multiple valid viewpoints while focusing on practical agreement.

### Tolerance for Disagreement
Quoting F. Scott Fitzgerald: "The test of a first-rate intelligence is the ability to hold two opposing ideas in mind at the same time and still keep the ability to function." Diversity of perspective is a feature, not a bug — but it must be channeled toward aligned outcomes.

### KPI Alignment Across Levels
"Higher level KPIs must align with lower level KPIs to ensure that the system does not drive incompatible behavior." Misaligned metrics at different organizational levels create teams that work against each other.

## Bob Moesta's Productive Arguments
Teams need to shift from egotistical arguments to productive debates about product development. This requires:
- Understanding the customer rather than defending personal opinions
- Focusing on empathy — what does the customer actually need?
- Accepting that "it's not about making this less hard; it's just hard, period"
- Using "Shape Up" methodology over rigid scrum/agile

## Michael Levin's Collective Intelligence Warning
While biological [Cognitive Glue](/concepts/cognitive-glue) principles can inspire team design, they must be tempered by human values. Forcing collective goals without individual consent produces "human rights disasters." Team alignment must respect individual agency and diverse perspectives.

## Ian's Context-Centered Design Canvas
Ian's evolving framework attempts to solve this by providing teams with a shared visual tool — the [Customer Context Map](/frameworks/customer-context-map) — that gives everyone a common reference point for discussing customer context. Instead of opinion battles, teams can ask: "What does the map tell us?"
